# Exile of House Vandris

## Chapter 1: The Betrayal

The Consortium's assassins came at night, during the dust storm season when Dur Kattan's orbital sensors were blinded. They had been smuggled onto Araxis inside water shipments — hiding in the recycling tanks of cargo vessels, breathing through miniature rebreathers for the seventy-two-hour journey from orbit to surface.

Lady Senna was their primary target. She survived because of a decision she had made three years earlier: to sleep in the Araxeen quarter of the city rather than the offworld compound. The assassins killed forty-seven members of House Vandris's household staff and destroyed the communications array. By the time Kael reached his mother, the compound was burning.

"We go below," Lady Senna said, and for the first time Kael heard genuine fear in her voice. Not for herself — she had made peace with death decades ago — but for the alliance. If House Vandris fell, the Keepers would lose their only ally in the Hegemony, and the sandweavers' warning would die unheard.

## Chapter 2: Life Below

The cave network became their home. Three hundred and twelve survivors of House Vandris descended into the Araxeen underground, guided by Dhamira and a company of Keepers who had been preparing for exactly this scenario.

Life below was alien in every sense. The bioluminescent chronodust cast everything in amber light. The temperature was a constant 24 degrees — comfortable but disorienting after years of Araxis's brutal surface extremes. Sound traveled strangely through the crystalline walls, carrying whispers from kilometers away.

The sandweavers were a constant presence. Juvenile weavers, ranging from one to five meters in length, moved through side tunnels with a rhythmic pulsing motion that generated a low hum — a sound the Keepers called the Breath. It was, Kael realized through his Resonance practice, not merely mechanical vibration. It was communication. The weavers were aware of the humans in their network, and they were — for reasons no one fully understood — tolerant.

"They remember," Dhamira explained. "The network has memory. It remembers that the Keepers have maintained the caves for ten thousand years. It remembers that you brought the codex translation. In its way, the network is grateful."

## Chapter 3: The Deep Resonance

Exile forced Kael deeper into Resonance practice. Without the distractions of governance and war, he could devote entire days to the discipline. Dhamira pushed him past every boundary he had previously encountered.

The breakthrough came on the two hundred and third day below. Kael achieved what the Keepers called Full Resonance — a state in which his individual consciousness merged temporarily with the sandweaver network. The experience was beyond language.

He perceived Araxis not as a planet but as an organism. The sandweavers were its circulatory system, the chronodust its blood, the cave network its nervous tissue. The surface desert was skin — protective, harsh, necessary. And beneath it all, a slow, vast intelligence observed the universe with a patience measured in geological epochs.

The intelligence was aware of the substrate fractures. It was aware of the Hegemony, the war, the burning of its healing substrate. And it was afraid.

"It is not angry," Kael told his mother afterward, still trembling. "It is not vengeful. It is afraid. It has seen this before — in its home system, twelve thousand years ago. It knows what comes next."

What came next, the sandweaver intelligence showed him, was a cascade failure that would render fold travel impossible across the entire galactic arm. Not in centuries. In years.
